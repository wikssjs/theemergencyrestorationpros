Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, there is no single statewide “general contractor” license requirement for water damage restoration, but restoration work is still governed by overlapping standards and disposal rules that can affect what can be done and when. The first baseline most insurers expect is adherence to IICRC standards: S500 for water damage and S520 for mold. Even if a company does not explicitly advertise the standards, your documentation should still reflect drying goals, monitoring, and (when applicable) mold assessment and clearance testing.

For pre-1978 homes, lead safety is a big deal. Under the EPA Lead RRP Rule, any contractor disturbing painted surfaces must have EPA Lead-Safe Certification and a Certified Renovator on site when lead paint could be impacted. Delaware County’s older housing stock makes this more likely in Sharon Hill than in newer builds.

For sewage and floodwater, Pennsylvania’s disposal requirements come into play. Category 3 projects involve biohazardous waste handling and require compliant waste disposal under PA DEP Chapter 78 rules (how waste is managed, transported, and disposed). Building permits may also be required for repairs—especially if you remove structural elements, perform electrical/plumbing work as part of the restoration, or replace large sections of drywall/plaster that affect building systems.

To protect yourself, ask contractors for: (a) their IICRC credentials (for appropriate scope), (b) certificate of insurance naming your property address, (c) EPA Lead-Safe Certification where pre-1978 surfaces may be disturbed, and (d) proof of workers’ compensation coverage or an approved exemption. In Pennsylvania, you can verify workers’ comp through WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database). A reputable contractor will also tell you what typically needs permits versus what is usually done under emergency mitigation (extraction, containment, drying equipment setup, and restoration sequencing).

Homeowner tip: start with emergency mitigation immediately, then confirm permitting needs before demolition-heavy repairs begin.

Water damage class and category — what Sharon Hill homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Restoration contractors classify damage by both CLASS (extent of saturation) and CATEGORY (contamination level of the water). Understanding these distinctions helps homeowners in Sharon Hill evaluate contractor recommendations, understand why costs vary, and decide what can be saved versus what should be removed. In a community where about 94.7% of homes were built before 1980, older plaster and hardwood often absorb and hold moisture differently than modern materials, so you may see more “hidden” wetness inside wall assemblies and flooring (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024).

In Delaware County housing, Class 3 and Class 4 conditions are not unusual after supply-line failures or prolonged leaks—especially when water migrates into insulation and framing and when plaster layers take time to dry. CATEGORY also changes treatment. A supply-line burst may be handled as Category 1, which focuses on extraction and drying. A sewage backup is Category 3 and triggers full biohazard procedures, including disposal of porous materials that cannot be properly decontaminated.

A practical way to think about cost: if your scenario is Class 2 with Category 1 (a full room but clean water), you might land near the $800–$7,000 structural drying band depending on materials and drying goals. But if the same room becomes Category 3 due to sewage or contaminated floodwater, the job can jump substantially because you’re paying for biohazard protocol and disposal on top of drying—often pushing total project cost toward the upper end of relevant bands and increasing repair scope.
