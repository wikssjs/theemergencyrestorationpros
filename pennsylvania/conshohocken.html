Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, water damage and mold remediation work sits at the intersection of industry standards and disposal/safety rules—so homeowners in Conshohocken should ask what standards and permits apply to the work actually being done. Pennsylvania doesn’t require a general contractor licence at the state level, but that doesn’t mean “anything goes.” Restoration tasks are typically governed by (1) IICRC standards used by insurers and (2) environmental/safety requirements for lead paint disturbance and sewage waste.

Here’s what commonly applies:

  • IICRC S500 (water damage) and S520 (mold): industry baseline that many insurance carriers expect.
  • EPA Lead RRP Rule: for pre-1978 homes, if the project disturbs painted surfaces (common when cutting out affected drywall/plaster or removing baseboards), the contractor must be EPA Lead-Safe Certified and must use a Certified Renovator onsite. Use certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov to verify.
  • PA DEP Chapter 78: biohazardous/sewage waste disposal rules for Category 3 cleanup.
  • Building permits (municipal): usually required for structural repairs and for electrical/plumbing work performed alongside restoration—often also when replacements affect load-bearing elements. Check with your specific Conshohocken-area municipality for permit triggers.
  • Workers’ compensation: PA requires employers to carry workers’ comp; verify through WCAIS (PA’s workers’ comp database).

Before signing, verify step-by-step: confirm IICRC certification for the drying/mold lead (ask for certificates), request certificate of insurance naming your address (general liability and workers’ comp where applicable), check EPA Lead-Safe/RRP credentials for pre-1978 disturbance, and confirm workers’ comp coverage or the contractor’s exemption documentation. You should also ask the contractor to separate what is mitigation (typically no building permit) versus what is reconstruction/electrical/plumbing (often permit-dependent).

Water damage class and category — what Conshohocken homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Restoration contractors classify damage by Class (extent of saturation) and Category (contamination level). Understanding these terms helps Conshohocken homeowners evaluate recommendations, compare quotes, and see why one contractor might propose containment and testing while another focuses only on drying. In Conshohocken’s housing stock—where 61.1% of homes were built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024)—Class 3 and 4 conditions are common after pipe bursts because older plaster and hardwood systems absorb water differently, and moisture can travel deeper into subfloor assemblies and masonry.

When quotes differ, it’s usually because the contractor evaluated one of these classifications differently—or because the water degraded over time. Here’s a simple dollar example: if you catch a clean supply-line leak early (Category 1) you may stay near $800–$5,000 for extraction and emergency drying. If that same leak sits for long enough to create widespread wet insulation and affected assemblies (moving toward higher Class), the job can shift toward the $1,000–$10,000 structural drying range due to additional equipment time and more monitoring visits. And if the source is sewage backup (Category 3), even a “smaller” area often costs more because disposal of porous materials and biohazard protocols are required regardless of square footage.

Use Class/category language to ask better questions: “What Class did you measure with moisture mapping?” and “What Category are you treating for, based on source and sanitation needs?” That’s the fastest way to spot whether a proposal matches your actual risk.
