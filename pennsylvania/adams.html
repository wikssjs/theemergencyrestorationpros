Water damage class and category — what Adams homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Restoration contractors classify damage by both CLASS (extent of saturation into materials) and CATEGORY (contamination level of the water). Understanding these distinctions helps Adams homeowners evaluate contractor recommendations, compare bids, and understand why “similar” losses can end up in different cost ranges. In Adams, where about 15.3% of housing was built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), Class 3 and 4 damage is more likely after pipe bursts because older plaster, hardwood, and masonry assemblies absorb and retain moisture differently than modern drywall and lightweight floor coverings. That can turn a localized leak into a broader drying project once moisture migrates into framing, subfloors, and concealed wall cavities.

Here’s where cost clarity comes in: Category 1 events are generally handled with standard drying and sanitation, while Category 3 (sewage/floodwater) requires biohazard protocols and disposal of contaminated porous materials. Drying timelines reflect Class. For instance, a small Class 1 event may stabilize within a few days and land near the $800–$4,500 range when response is fast. But if the damage is Class 3 or 4—especially when hardwood or plaster must be actively dried with careful monitoring—the project may require extended equipment time and selective demolition, pushing costs into different bands even when the water is “clean.”

Bottom line: ask your contractor to explain the CLASS and CATEGORY they’re using and what that means for equipment duration, containment (if needed), and what materials they plan to salvage versus remove.
