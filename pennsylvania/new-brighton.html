Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

Pennsylvania doesn’t require a general contractor license at the state level, but water damage and mold remediation are still governed by overlapping standards and disposal rules that affect how work must be performed. For New Brighton homeowners, the biggest “gotchas” usually involve (1) what’s disturbed, (2) how sewage waste is handled, and (3) whether lead paint compliance is triggered.

At the industry baseline, restoration should follow IICRC standards: S500 for water damage and S520 for mold. For pre-1978 homes (a large share of New Brighton’s stock, given the 93.0% pre-1980 profile), the EPA Lead Renovation, Repair and Painting (Lead RRP) Rule applies if contractors disturb painted surfaces. That means you should expect a Certified Renovator on site and EPA Lead-Safe Certification—verify on the EPA’s certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov.

For Category 3 work, biohazardous/sewage waste disposal is governed by PA DEP Chapter 78. Your contractor must use appropriate containment, cleaning, and disposal practices for contaminated porous materials. Permits are typically required for the more invasive parts of restoration, not for basic extraction and drying—for example, structural repairs, drywall replacement that affects load-bearing elements, and any electrical or plumbing work done in conjunction with reconstruction. Finally, Pennsylvania requires employers to carry workers’ compensation; check via the WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database).

To verify credentials step-by-step in New Brighton: (1) ask for the IICRC certificates relevant to S500/S520; (2) request EPA Lead-Safe proof and the lead technician certificate if pre-1978 surfaces are disturbed; (3) confirm PA workers’ comp coverage (or an exemption) using WCAIS; and (4) review the certificate of insurance to ensure it names your property address as additional insured where required by your policy/contract. Then ask what portions of your job require permits locally and when the contractor plans to pull them.

Water damage class and category — what New Brighton homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Restoration contractors classify damage by both CLASS (extent of saturation) and CATEGORY (contamination level). Understanding these distinctions helps New Brighton homeowners evaluate contractor recommendations, understand why costs vary, and make smarter salvage-or-replace decisions before demolition starts.

In New Brighton’s 93.0% pre-1980 housing stock (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), Class 3 and 4 conditions are more common after leaks, because older plaster walls, hardwood floors, and masonry can absorb water in ways that aren’t captured by “how it looks.” Once water reaches insulation, wall cavities, or under subfloor layers, drying becomes more technical: LGR dehumidification, air movement, and moisture mapping must be planned to bring materials back to acceptable moisture targets.

Budget differences are easiest to see with real decision points. For example, if a leak only impacts a small area (Class 1), homeowners may stay in the $1,300–$4,500 range for water extraction + emergency drying when addressed quickly. But if the same leak saturates plaster and subflooring across multiple rooms (Class 2 or higher), drying time expands and costs can move into the $1,500–$12,000 structural drying band. If sewage or prolonged floodwater triggers Category 3, the project can also require biohazard protocol and disposal practices—shifting scope beyond standard drying.

Bottom line: ask your contractor to explain both the class and category, show the moisture map, and tie the price to the measured drying path. That’s how you avoid paying for the wrong scope.

What is Category 3 (black water) damage?

Category 3 in Pennsylvania restoration terms refers to black water sources such as sewage backups, floodwater from outside, and situations where water has been standing long enough to become heavily contaminated (often referenced as 72+ hours). In New Brighton, this is a common concern after sewer issues or sump failures during wet weather. Category 3 is treated as a biohazard, so the process includes a full protocol: extraction, controlled cleaning, disinfection, and careful disposal of porous materials that can’t be reliably decontaminated (like certain insulation, some drywall, and damaged wood). Costs are typically higher than basic clean water because of the PPE, containment practices, and disposal requirements; Category 3 cleanup in this market commonly falls in the $1,200–$6,000 range depending on how much material must be removed.
