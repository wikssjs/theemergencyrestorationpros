Water damage class and category — what Elizabethtown homeowners need to know

In Elizabethtown, restoration decisions are driven by two systems: CLASS (how far the water has spread and how much has penetrated) and CATEGORY (how contaminated the water is). CLASS helps you understand extent—whether moisture is mostly in the air and surface layers, or whether it has soaked into walls, subfloors, and insulation. CATEGORY tells you what health controls are required. Together, they explain why two “wet basements” can cost dramatically different amounts.

Elizabethtown’s housing stock is a key reason CLASS becomes important: with about 67.9% of homes built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), plaster, hardwood, and older masonry assemblies can absorb and hold water longer than modern drywall-and-LVP layouts. That makes Class 3 and Class 4 losses common after pipe bursts or ceiling leaks—especially when water travels downward and spreads through older wall cavities. In these scenarios, contractors often need longer drying time and more intensive monitoring to reach drying goals, and sometimes selective demolition if moisture cannot be safely dried in place.

Here’s a practical money example: if a clean supply-line leak stays in a smaller, limited area (often nearer to Class 1) and responds quickly, you might remain in the $600–$4,500 extraction/drying band. But if that same leak turns into extensive wall and subfloor saturation that requires specialty drying (Class 4 patterns), the “drying phase” can justify a move into higher structural drying costs, often within the $1,200–$8,000 range depending on materials and equipment run time.

Finally, Category 3 (sewage/floodwater) increases costs even if visible saturation looks similar, because biohazard cleaning, antimicrobial steps, and disposal of porous materials are required under applicable Pennsylvania rules.
