Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, water damage restoration is shaped by overlapping standards and disposal rules rather than one single permit process. While PA does not require a general contractor license at the state level, certain aspects of the work are governed by requirements that affect how contractors plan, document, and dispose of materials.

First, the industry baseline is IICRC. IICRC S500 (water damage) and S520 (mold) are the standards most insurers look for when a claim involves drying verification or mold cleanup. Second, lead safety can apply. Under EPA’s Lead RRP Rule, if your home is pre-1978 and the restoration requires disturbing painted surfaces that may contain lead, the contractor must have EPA Lead-Safe Certification and the right lead practices on-site. You can verify certification using the EPA Certified Renovator search (certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov).

Third, Category 3 disposal is regulated. PA DEP Chapter 78 governs biohazardous/sewage waste disposal for Category 3 projects. Fourth, building permits can be required for reconstruction (not routine drying) when you’re removing/replacing drywall in a way that affects load-bearing elements, doing electrical/plumbing work, or performing other structural repairs. Finally, workers’ compensation matters. Employers in Pennsylvania are required to carry workers’ comp; homeowners should confirm coverage using the WCAIS lookup or ask for proof/exemption.

For your East Hempfield job, ask for (a) IICRC credentials for the lead technician/lead drying work (as applicable), (b) a certificate of insurance naming your address as appropriate, (c) EPA Lead-Safe certification for pre-1978 homes before any surface disturbance, and (d) workers’ comp proof or exemption documentation. Then verify online before work starts—credentials and coverage are the easiest way to prevent delays after mitigation begins.

Water damage class and category — what East Hempfield homeowners need to know

East Hempfield homeowners should know that restoration companies sort losses using two terms: CLASS (how widespread the moisture saturation is inside materials) and CATEGORY (the contamination level of the water). These classifications shape both health precautions and cost. For example, in Lancaster County’s older housing stock—where about 40.5% of homes were built before 1980—Class 3 and Class 4 situations are not uncommon after plumbing failures or storms because plaster and hardwood absorb water differently than newer drywall and floating floors. That means the “dry area” you can see may be only the surface of a much bigger moisture migration problem under baseboards, in wall cavities, and into subfloor layers.

The category is also critical for what you pay for. Category 1 (clean water) focuses on extraction and controlled drying. Category 3 (black water) triggers biohazard protocols, antimicrobial disinfection where applicable, and higher disposal and material handling costs. The practical takeaway: if a contractor recommends tearing out more material, it’s usually because the class indicates deeper saturation—not because of upselling.

A simple dollar example: if you catch a supply-line leak early and it stays mostly in a small, minimally absorbed area (Class 1, Category 1), you may stay closer to $600–$4,500 for extraction + emergency drying. But if the same incident is delayed and progresses to deeper saturation (for instance, Class 3 in a basement ceiling with fully wetted insulation) the drying plan and monitoring timeline typically expand, and the price can shift upward into the structural drying band such as $1,200–$8,000, even without confirmed mold.
