Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, restoration work is shaped by overlapping standards and disposal rules rather than one single “permit process.” While Pennsylvania does not require a general contractor license at the state level, water damage and mold remediation are still governed by nationally recognized work standards and state requirements that affect how jobs are performed and documented.

First, IICRC standards are the industry baseline: IICRC S500 for water damage and IICRC S520 for mold. Insurers commonly expect work to align with these standards when a claim is filed. Second, for pre-1978 homes, the EPA Lead Renovation, Repair and Painting (Lead RRP) Rule matters: if lead paint surfaces are disturbed, a Certified Renovator must be on site and contractors must be EPA Lead-Safe Certified. Homeowners can verify credentials using the EPA certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov site.

Third, PA DEP Chapter 78 governs sewage waste disposal for Category 3 projects. Fourth, building permits are typically required when work involves structural repairs, drywall replacement that affects load-bearing elements, or electrical/plumbing work performed along with restoration. For exact triggers in New Kensington and nearby Westmoreland County municipalities, confirm with your local building department.

Finally, workers’ compensation: PA requires employers to carry workers’ comp; verify via WCAIS (the PA workers’ comp database). Before signing, ask for (a) the IICRC certificate/credentials for the lead technician if applicable, (b) insurance certificate naming the homeowner’s address/interest, (c) EPA Lead-Safe Certification for pre-1978 homes, and (d) workers’ comp proof or an approved exemption. To verify step-by-step: check online IICRC listings if offered by the contractor, confirm the EPA lead renovator/firm status, request the COI with policy details, and verify workers’ comp status in WCAIS—then compare what’s on paper to what’s promised in the written scope.

Water damage class and category — what New Kensington homeowners need to know

New Kensington homeowners should treat “water damage” as two separate concepts: CLASS and CATEGORY. CLASS describes the extent of saturation and how deeply water penetrated materials (how far it traveled into walls, insulation, and subfloors). CATEGORY describes contamination level—whether the water is clean, grey, or black. Contractors in Westmoreland County use these distinctions to justify extraction, controlled demolition, disinfection, and how aggressively they must dry and monitor.

In New Kensington’s older housing stock—91.1% of homes built before 1980—Class 3 and Class 4 situations come up after pipe bursts because plaster and hardwood absorb moisture differently than many modern wall systems. For example, plaster can trap moisture behind the face, while hardwood over plank subfloors can hold moisture longer and distort if humidity control is rushed. That’s why drying time can extend from a few days to well over a week when deep saturation is discovered.

Here’s a concrete way costs can differ. A small localized leak that fits Class 1 often lands in the $1,200–$6,000 extraction + emergency drying band because the affected area can be dried quickly and monitored until target moisture goals are met. On the other hand, deep saturation involving masonry, hardwood, or plaster (Class 4) may demand specialty structural drying and more controlled dehumidification, which is why typical ranges can shift into the $4,000–$5,000 territory even before reconstruction begins. This difference isn’t “upselling”—it reflects how long moisture remains in the structure and how much controlled drying is required.

Understanding class/category also helps homeowners decide what should be salvaged. The goal is always to dry to documented targets first, then determine whether contents and building materials can return safely or require removal.
