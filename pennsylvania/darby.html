Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, water damage and mold remediation aren’t handled under a single rule—there are overlapping standards that govern how work must be done and how waste must be disposed of. While Pennsylvania does not require a general contractor licence at the state level, that doesn’t mean “no rules.” For restoration and demolition, the key frameworks homeowners should understand are: (1) IICRC industry standards, (2) lead-safety requirements for older homes, (3) biohazard/sewage disposal rules for Category 3 losses, and (4) local permitting when structural or electrical/plumbing work is involved.

First, IICRC S500 (water damage) and S520 (mold) are the industry baseline that many insurers expect to see documented. Second, because many Darby homes are pre-1978, the EPA Lead RRP Rule applies when contractors disturb painted surfaces that could contain lead—meaning an EPA Certified Renovator must be involved on-site and firms must be EPA Lead-Safe Certified. Homeowners can confirm certification using certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov. For Category 3 sewage backup cleanup, PA DEP Chapter 78 governs biohazardous/sewage waste disposal.

Permits depend on what’s actually being removed or modified. In practice, permits are commonly required for structural repairs, drywall replacement that affects load-bearing elements, and any electrical/plumbing work done alongside restoration. Homeowners should also expect workers’ compensation coverage requirements—employers in PA must carry workers’ comp, which you can verify through WCAIS (PA’s workers’ compensation database).

To verify credentials step-by-step, ask for (a) IICRC certificate for the lead technician (if lead-safe work is possible), (b) certificate of insurance naming your address/coverage holder as applicable, (c) EPA Lead-Safe Certification for pre-1978 homes, and (d) PA workers’ comp proof or exemption documentation. Then cross-check insurance and workers’ comp status before demolition begins.

Water damage class and category — what Darby homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Restoration contractors classify damage using two concepts: Class (the extent of saturation and how far water traveled) and Category (the contamination level of the water). Understanding these distinctions helps Darby homeowners evaluate contractor recommendations, spot upsells, and understand why costs vary—from the drying equipment time needed to whether mold remediation or biohazard protocols are mandatory. With Darby’s housing stock skewing older—about 93.5% built before 1980—Class 3 and 4 situations are more common after leaks because plaster and hardwood assemblies can hold and transmit moisture differently than modern drywall and vinyl flooring.

Here’s a practical way to think about it: Category drives health risk and cleanup rules, while Class drives drying complexity. For example, a supply-line leak may be Category 1 but still be Class 2 or Class 3 if water reaches wall cavities and the subfloor. Conversely, a sewage event is often Category 3 even if the “visible” area seems limited—because waste contamination can permeate porous materials. In Darby, the difference between Class 2 and Class 3 often shows up as longer drying time, more monitoring points, and selective demolition if plaster delaminates.

Dollar example: if you see a quote that stays in the $800–$7,000 structural drying band for a situation described as soaked wall cavities (Class 3), it may not be accounting for the additional drying time and monitoring usually required. But if the source water is clean (Category 1) and the affected materials are limited, a Class 2 drying plan closer to the mid-range is often justified. The key is for the contractor to document class, category, moisture readings, and drying goals—not just estimate based on square footage.
