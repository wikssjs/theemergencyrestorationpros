Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, water damage and mold remediation work is governed by overlapping standards—some are industry baselines insurers expect, while others are federal and state-level rules that affect how contractors handle contaminated waste and lead paint. Pennsylvania does not require a general contractor license at the state level, but that doesn’t mean “no rules.”

First, IICRC S500 (water damage) and IICRC S520 (mold) serve as the industry baseline for how drying and mold work should be performed; reputable firms use these protocols for documentation and drying goals. Second, if your home was built before 1978 (important for East Hanover’s 42.3% pre-1980 housing stock), the EPA Lead RRP Rule can apply: any disturbance of lead paint surfaces generally requires an EPA Lead-Safe Certified Renovator on site. Contractors can verify eligibility via certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov.

For Category 3 sewage or floodwater, contractors must follow PA DEP Chapter 78 requirements for biohazardous/sewage waste disposal. Structural rebuild permitting is typically required when repairs involve framing changes, quantity drywall replacement that affects assemblies, and electrical or HVAC work. Homeowners can also ask for workers’ compensation proof—PA requires employers to carry workers’ comp, which you can verify using WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database).

What usually needs a permit: electrical/plumbing changes, any load-bearing framing modifications, and rebuild work beyond simple drying. What often does NOT: containment and drying activities performed to stop loss and remediate contamination under approved protocols (still require compliance with waste/lead rules).

Step-by-step for East Hanover homeowners: (1) Ask for the contractor’s IICRC documentation (or certificate details) for the lead technician where applicable, (2) request a certificate of insurance naming your address as additional insured, (3) confirm EPA Lead-Safe Certification for pre-1978 homes, and (4) obtain workers’ comp proof or an exemption letter, then verify via WCAIS.

Water damage class and category — what East Hanover homeowners need to know

In East Hanover, water damage is categorized two ways: by Class (how deeply the water has absorbed into materials) and by Category (how contaminated the water is). Understanding both helps you evaluate contractor recommendations, gauge whether something can be salvaged or needs removal, and see why one estimate can be far higher than another. With Dauphin County’s older housing stock—where about 42.3% of homes were built before 1980—Class 3 and Class 4 events can be common after pipe bursts. Older plaster and hardwood often absorb water differently and hold moisture longer than modern drywall and many sheet vinyl or laminate assemblies, making drying more complex and often longer.

Let’s ground it in cost. A small, contained leak (for example, a localized ceiling stain with limited absorption) is often treated like a Class 1 loss and can fall into the practical extraction band of $1,200–$5,200 when you factor equipment and monitoring. But the moment water reaches subfloor cavities and multiple building layers (Class 3/4), you may be looking at specialized drying cycles and more aggressive monitoring—pushing costs toward the upper portion of structural drying (up to $12,000). If contamination is involved—such as sewage backup—Category 3 cleanup becomes a separate and more regulated scope due to PPE, disinfection, and porous material disposal, which is why Category 3 projects are commonly priced closer to $1,000–$5,000 even before reconstruction begins.

In short: Class explains how far moisture traveled; Category explains what health risks exist. Both must match the actual conditions measured on site—not just what the leak “looks like.”

How to choose a water damage restoration contractor in East Hanover

Next, get 2–3 itemized written quotes. The best quotes separate labor and materials, list the drying equipment plan, and state the monitoring frequency and drying goals (not just “dry it out”). Look for warranties and equipment standards. Ask for the workmanship warranty length and whether there’s coverage tied to the specific drying and containment activities. Finally, insist on timeline clarity. A credible crew provides an arrival estimate and a completion window in writing, including daily monitoring expectations for multi-day structural drying—especially important in older East Hanover homes where plaster and hardwood can extend dry-out.

After water damage in East Hanover, mold can show up even if the visible leak seems to be “over.” Look for musty odors that linger after drying, visible speckling or fuzzy growth on drywall seams, baseboards, and behind furniture, and bubbling or peeling paint that returns after minor drying attempts. What is Category 3 (black water) damage?

Category 3, sometimes called “black water,” is the most contaminated category. In East Hanover and across Dauphin County, it commonly includes sewage backups, floodwater from outside, and water that has been standing for an extended period (often referenced as 72+ hours) because it becomes contaminated and risky even if it started as something else.
