Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, restoration work can involve multiple overlapping requirements depending on what was damaged and what materials must be disturbed. Pennsylvania does not require a general contractor license at the state level, but the compliance burden is real for water and mold work—especially in older Horsham homes.

At the industry baseline, IICRC standards are widely used: S500 for water damage and S520 for mold. Many insurers expect work to align with these standards even if permits aren’t required for the mitigation itself. If your home was built before 1978, the EPA Lead Renovation, Repair and Painting (Lead RRP) Rule can apply when restoration requires disturbing paint surfaces—meaning the contractor must be EPA Lead-Safe Certified and use a Certified Renovator on site. You can verify certifications through the certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov tool.

For Category 3 (sewage/floodwater) projects, Pennsylvania DEP rules in Chapter 78 govern how biohazardous/sewage waste is handled and disposed of. Also, building permits may be required for structural repairs, drywall replacement involving load-bearing elements, and any electrical or plumbing work performed alongside restoration—check with Horsham Township and other local municipality departments for exact triggers.

To protect yourself, ask contractors for: (a) their IICRC certificate documentation, (b) certificate of insurance naming your address, (c) EPA Lead-Safe Certification for pre-1978 homes, and (d) proof of Pennsylvania workers’ compensation coverage or a valid exemption. Step-by-step: confirm certifications via online registries/certificates, verify the insurance certificate shows correct coverage, and check workers’ comp via PA’s WCAIS (or request their proof of coverage letter/exemption documentation). Only then should you sign an agreement for demolition, drying, or content handling that changes your property condition.

Water damage class and category — what Horsham homeowners need to know

Because Horsham has a meaningful share of older housing stock—about 47.9% built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024)—Class 3 and Class 4 scenarios show up more often after pipe bursts. Even if the visible water looks limited, hidden wet materials can drive longer drying times and sometimes selective removal. Category matters just as much: supply-line water is usually treated as Category 1, but if water sits long enough, comes from grey/black sources, or involves sewage backflow, the project can shift into remediation-level scope.

To ground this in price: if you catch a small, localized leak quickly, Class 1 drying may land in the $800–$5,000 range. But if the water penetrates walls and subfloor (Class 2), the drying phase can move toward $1,000–$10,000, and Class 3 deep saturation may justify higher-cost specialty drying and more extensive monitoring—often into $1,000–$14,000. That difference is typically justified because the goal isn’t “turning equipment on,” but reducing moisture to drying targets throughout affected materials.
