Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, there isn’t a single, simple “one-size” state permit for water damage restoration, but your project can intersect multiple rules depending on the work type and the age of the home. In general, water damage and mold remediation practices are anchored to industry standards—most insurers and experienced contractors expect work aligned to IICRC S500 (water damage) and IICRC S520 (mold). For Shaler homeowners, this matters because proper documentation and drying goals often determine both whether the problem is actually solved and whether a claim pays cleanly.

When lead paint could be disturbed, Pennsylvania projects also intersect federal requirements. If your home is pre-1978, work that disturbs lead paint surfaces requires an EPA Certified Renovator on site and contractors must be EPA Lead-Safe Certified. You can search certified professionals at certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov.

For Category 3 (sewage/black water), sewage and biohazardous waste disposal is governed by PA DEP Chapter 78. Building permits are typically needed only for the “rebuild” elements—structural repairs, drywall replacement affecting load-bearing elements, and any electrical/plumbing work performed alongside restoration. Drying itself usually doesn’t trigger a permit, but repairs can. Finally, PA requires employers to carry workers’ compensation; verify coverage using the WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database).

Step-by-step for Shaler: (1) ask for an IICRC certificate for the project lead/lead technician where applicable, (2) request certificate(s) of insurance naming your address as the certificate holder when appropriate, (3) confirm EPA Lead-Safe Certification for pre-1978 homes if demolition is needed, and (4) verify workers’ comp coverage or documented exemption via PA’s workers’ comp records. Get these before demolition so you’re not trapped mid-project.

Water damage class and category — what Shaler homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Restoration contractors classify damage by both CLASS (how much water has spread/absorbed into materials) and CATEGORY (how contaminated the water is). Understanding these distinctions helps Shaler homeowners evaluate recommendations, understand why costs vary, and decide what can be salvaged versus what should be removed and replaced.

In Shaler’s housing stock—where approximately 82.8% of homes were built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024)—it’s common to see Class 3 or Class 4 situations after pipe bursts or overflow because older plaster and hardwood often absorb moisture and release it slowly. Allegheny County’s seasonal conditions can also extend the time moisture lingers inside wall cavities and basement assemblies, especially when finished basements are present. The contractor’s job is to dry to measurable goals, not just “dry to the eye.”

A practical way to think about cost: Class drives drying scope and duration; category drives health controls. For example, a homeowner who calls early after a supply-line leak may see a job stay closer to $1,200–$3,500 for extraction and emergency drying (typical for less-absorbed, cleaner scenarios). If the same leak has soaked into multiple levels and isn’t addressed quickly—moving into deeper saturation—you may be closer to the $1,500–$12,000 structural drying band for longer LGR drying and monitoring until full drying goals are achieved. And if the water is sewage (Category 3), the health and disposal requirements can push the project into the higher end of sewage cleanup pricing because porous materials may need removal and antimicrobial/disinfection steps become mandatory.

What is Category 3 (black water) damage?
