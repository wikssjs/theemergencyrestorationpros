Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, you generally don’t need a statewide “general contractor license” just to perform restoration work. However, water damage and mold remediation are still governed by overlapping requirements that affect how work is done and what can be disturbed—especially in pre-1978 housing. For O'Hara homeowners, the practical rule is: if a job involves certain types of demolition, hazardous materials handling, or regulated waste disposal, you should expect added compliance steps.

First, drying and mold work should align with the IICRC standards most insurers and carriers rely on: S500 for water damage and S520 for mold remediation. Second, if your home was built before 1978, the EPA Lead Renovation, Repair and Painting (Lead RRP) Rule can apply when renovation disturbs lead-painted surfaces. In that case, a Certified Renovator must be on-site, and contractors should be EPA Lead-Safe Certified. You can check the registry at certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov.

For sewage issues (Category 3), biohazardous/sewage waste disposal is regulated under PA DEP Chapter 78. Permits are typically not required for drying itself, but they are commonly required for structural repairs, electrical/plumbing changes, and some drywall replacement when it involves more than simple non-load-bearing patches—municipal rules can vary by local building department.

Finally, verify workers’ compensation. Pennsylvania requires employers to carry workers’ comp; you can confirm coverage or exemption information through PA’s workers’ comp lookup via WCAIS. Before any demo starts, ask contractors for: proof of IICRC credentials (and lead technician certification where applicable), certificates of insurance naming your address as a covered location, and workers’ compensation proof or exemption documentation.

Water damage class and category — what O'Hara homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Restoration contractors classify damage by both CLASS (extent of saturation) and CATEGORY (contamination level of the water). Understanding these distinctions helps O'Hara homeowners evaluate recommendations, understand why prices differ, and decide what can often be salvaged without creating ongoing moisture or mold risk. In a neighborhood with older housing—about 70.3% of homes built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024)—you may see more Class 3 and 4 conditions after pipe bursts because plaster and hardwood can absorb and redistribute moisture into wall cavities, subfloors, and baseboard areas.

Category tells you what health and containment work is required. Category 1 (clean) often results in standard extraction and emergency drying, while Category 3 (sewage/black water) triggers biohazard protocols, disinfection, and disposal of porous materials. Class tells you how deep the drying must go. A Class 2 event may be contained to an entire room, while Class 3 saturation can involve insulation and saturated materials moving deeper into assemblies, increasing drying time and monitoring. Class 4 is deep saturation (think hardwood, concrete, plaster), which usually requires specialty drying and longer monitoring—this is where remediation choices can save money later by preventing hidden microbial growth.

For a simple example, if you have a localized leak that stays closer to Category 1 and Class 1–2, emergency extraction and drying commonly lands in the $1,200–$3,500 range. If the same incident is delayed and shifts to Class 3 with added mold risk, the scope often moves toward the $1,500–$12,000 structural drying band—even before any separate mold remediation is considered.
