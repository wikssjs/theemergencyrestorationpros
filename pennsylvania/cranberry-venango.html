Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, you usually won’t deal with a single, simple licensing rule for water damage restoration—but there are several overlapping requirements that affect how work is performed and what needs permits. First, IICRC S500 (water damage) and S520 (mold) function as the industry baseline and are often what insurers expect for proper mitigation and documentation. For homes built before 1978, lead paint rules also come into play: if a contractor must disturb painted surfaces, the EPA Lead RRP requirements require EPA Lead-Safe Certification and, when applicable, a Certified Renovator on site for the lead-related work. Homeowners can verify certification through the EPA’s Certified Renovator search at https://certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov.

For sewage and floodwater, Category 3 work must follow state biohazard/sewage disposal rules under PA DEP Chapter 78, which can affect how porous materials are bagged, removed, and disposed of. Permits depend on the scope: emergency drying and most mitigation is typically exempt from major building permits, but structural repairs, drywall replacement that impacts load-bearing elements, and any electrical or plumbing work often require coordination with the local municipality’s code office.

Finally, Pennsylvania requires employers to carry workers’ compensation—so verify coverage or the contractor’s approved exemption. You can check workers’ comp status through PA’s WCAIS system.

What to ask in Cranberry: (1) IICRC documentation for drying/mold work, (2) EPA Lead-Safe credentials for pre-1978 homes, (3) a certificate of insurance naming the homeowner’s address where appropriate, and (4) proof of workers’ comp coverage. When in doubt, request the COI and WC verification before work begins so your claim and liability stay cleaner.

Water damage class and category — what Cranberry homeowners need to know

Cranberry’s older housing stock—where 68.9% of homes were built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024)—makes class outcomes more important. Older plaster and hardwood assemblies often absorb water into walls, framing, and subfloors rather than staying limited to the surface. That means events that might look manageable at first can become Class 2, Class 3, or even Class 4 if moisture reaches deeper layers.

Category drives health risk and scope. A basement supply line leak is usually considered Category 1 (clean water). Grey water (Category 2) can include washing machine overflow, with moderate contamination and extra sanitation steps. Sewage backup is Category 3 (black water), requiring full biohazard protocol and disposal of porous materials.

A practical dollar example: if a leak turns into a broader Class 3 scenario, drying commonly lands closer to the $800–$14,000 range for drying-related work, while a smaller Class 1 event may stay near the $900–$5,500 extraction and emergency drying band. That difference is justified when moisture mapping shows the water has migrated into cavities and when slower LGR drying is needed to protect plaster keys and hardwood.

What is Category 3 (black water) damage?

Category 3, often called “black water,” is water that is highly contaminated and includes sewage backup, floodwater from outside, or water that has been standing long enough (commonly 72+ hours) to develop serious microbial contamination. In Pennsylvania, Category 3 work is treated as biohazard cleanup: contractors typically use PPE, containment when needed, disinfecting, and removal/disposal of porous materials that cannot be safely decontaminated. This is one reason pricing changes dramatically compared to clean-water losses. While Category 1 extraction and emergency drying commonly ranges from $900–$5,500, sewage backup cleanup (Category 3) is commonly $1,000–$6,500 before restoration begins, due to the increased labor, disposal requirements, and sanitation steps. In Cranberry’s older housing stock, porous materials like insulation, wall coverings, and sometimes portions of plaster assemblies can be especially sensitive to prolonged contamination. If you suspect Category 3, treat it as urgent and avoid running fans that could spread contaminants without proper containment and protocol.
