Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

Pennsylvania doesn’t require a general contractor licence at the state level, but water damage and mold remediation work in the state is still shaped by overlapping standards and disposal rules that affect how contractors scope, document, and handle waste. For homeowners in Derry, it’s worth knowing what’s “standard” versus what can trigger permit or compliance steps.

First, industry baseline standards matter. Most insurers and reputable restoration firms follow IICRC S500 for water damage and IICRC S520 for mold-related work. Second, if your home is pre-1978, the EPA Lead RRP Rule is critical: contractors must be EPA Lead-Safe Certified when they disturb lead paint surfaces, and they must have a Certified Renovator on site. You can search certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov.

Third, for Category 3 sewage events, disposal and handling of biohazardous materials are governed through PA DEP Chapter 78 requirements. Fourth, building permits may be required for structural repairs, drywall replacement that affects load-bearing elements, or any electrical/plumbing work done alongside restoration—check with your municipality within Derry’s local government structure.

Finally, PA requires most employers to carry workers’ compensation; verify via WCAIS (PA’s workers’ comp database).

Ask contractors to provide: (a) IICRC certificates relevant to the work, (b) proof of insurance naming your address or premises, (c) EPA Lead-Safe Certification and the Certified Renovator credential (for pre-1978 homes), and (d) workers’ comp proof or exemption documentation. A trustworthy contractor will show you these documents before starting demolition or remediation.

  • For IICRC: request the technician’s certificate number and confirm it aligns with the scope (water vs mold).
  • For EPA Lead: ensure they’re Lead-Safe Certified and that a Certified Renovator is on site for disturbance.
  • For insurance: request a certificate of insurance that lists your location as the insured jobsite (or provides job-specific endorsement).
  • For workers’ comp: check for proof of coverage (policy numbers) or a valid exemption.
  • For permits: ask what permit(s) are needed for your specific repairs and which contractor handles them.
  • For sewage disposal: request a description of how contaminated materials are handled and disposed.

Water damage class and category — what Derry homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Restoration contractors classify damage by both CLASS (extent of saturation) and CATEGORY (contamination level of the water). For Derry homeowners, these labels help you evaluate recommendations, understand why some quotes include demolition and monitoring while others don’t, and make smarter decisions about what can be salvaged versus what should be removed.

In Derry’s housing context—where about 69.3% of the area’s dwellings were built before 1980—Class 3 and Class 4 situations can be more common after pipe bursts and roof leaks. Older plaster and dimensional lumber absorb and release moisture differently than modern drywall assemblies, and that can change drying timelines and sometimes require specialty drying equipment.

Here’s a practical example: if a small kitchen leak is caught early, it may be treated as Class 1 with Category 1 water, often in the $1,500–$5,500 range for extraction and emergency drying. But if the same leak runs longer and wicks into wall cavities and subfloor, you may be facing Class 2 or even Class 3 drying. That’s where costs can grow toward $800–$9,000 for structural drying-only follow-up, because the job now includes more materials, longer monitoring, and additional dehumidification.

Category then influences health and containment. Category 3 (sewage/floodwater) typically means biohazard protocol and disposal of porous materials. Even if the leak looks “contained,” the classification affects how technicians protect your home during cleanup and how thoroughly materials are cleaned or removed.
