Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, water damage and mold work is largely governed by standards and safety/disposal rules rather than a single statewide “restoration contractor license.” While a general contractor license isn’t universally required at the state level, DuBois homeowners should know that multiple requirements can apply depending on what’s being cleaned, removed, or repaired.

  • IICRC S500 (water damage) and S520 (mold) provide the industry baseline for drying and mold work. Many insurers expect these protocols to be followed.
  • EPA Lead RRP Rule: if the home is pre-1978, any renovation that disturbs lead-painted surfaces requires a Certified Renovator on-site and the contractor must be EPA Lead-Safe Certified. Use certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov to verify.
  • PA DEP Chapter 78 governs how biohazardous/sewage waste (Category 3) must be handled and disposed.
  • Building permits: permits are typically required for structural repairs, certain drywall replacement involving load-bearing elements, and any electrical/plumbing work done in conjunction with restoration. Check with your DuBois or Clearfield County building office.
  • Workers’ compensation: employers must carry workers’ comp in PA (verify via WCAIS), unless an exemption applies.

Step-by-step verification for DuBois homeowners: (1) Request the contractor’s IICRC documentation and confirm certification details; (2) verify EPA Lead-Safe/RRP for any pre-1978 disturbance through the EPA registry; (3) ask for certificate of insurance naming your address as applicable and confirm active coverage; (4) confirm workers’ comp proof or exemption documentation; (5) request the written scope and identify whether any permit-triggering repair work is planned (electrical, plumbing, structural). This helps prevent delays after the fact.

Water damage class and category — what DuBois homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Restoration contractors classify damage by both CLASS (extent of saturation) and CATEGORY (contamination level of the water). Understanding these distinctions helps DuBois homeowners evaluate contractor recommendations, understand why costs vary, and decide what can be salvaged versus what should be removed.

In DuBois’s housing stock—where 86.5% of homes were built before 1980—Class 3 and Class 4 losses are common after pipe bursts because older plaster walls and hardwood floors absorb water differently and can become saturated deeper than homeowners expect. In practice, this changes the drying approach: more LGR dehumidification, more monitoring points, and sometimes selective opening/drying of wall cavities or under-floor spaces. Meanwhile, Category determines safety steps. A small clean spill might fall into Category 1, but if water is contaminated by sewage backup or water standing long enough to support growth, Category 3 remediation protocols may apply.

Here’s a concrete cost example to clarify decisions: two homes with similar room size can show very different totals. A Class 2, Category 1 loss is often managed in the general $1,500–$8,000 zone depending on duration and materials, while a Class 3/4 situation in older plaster/hardwood—where deep saturation requires specialty drying and more labor—can move into higher ranges such as $4,000–$14,000 when remediation/testing or extensive drying is needed. The right classification keeps you from paying for unnecessary demolition—or from paying too little and having moisture/mold return.
