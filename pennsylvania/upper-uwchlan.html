Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, emergency water mitigation is usually treated differently than reconstruction, and the rules can overlap. Pennsylvania doesn’t have a single, universal “state water damage restoration license” you must hold to start drying work, but the work is still governed by standards and disposal/lead requirements that contractors must follow.

Key items homeowners should know:

  • IICRC S500 (water damage) and S520 (mold) are the industry baseline that many insurers expect when classifying losses, documenting moisture, and performing remediation.
  • EPA Lead RRP Rule: if your home was built before 1978, the contractor must be EPA Lead-Safe Certified and use appropriate lead-safe practices when disturbing lead paint surfaces. You can search certified renovators at certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov.
  • PA DEP Chapter 78 governs biohazardous/sewage waste disposal. Category 3 jobs must follow regulated disposal practices for contaminated materials.
  • Building permits: permits are commonly required for reconstruction—for example, electrical/plumbing work and structural repairs or drywall replacement that affects load-bearing elements. Check with your Upper Uwchlan-area municipality for specific permit triggers.
  • Workers’ compensation: PA requires covered employers to carry workers’ comp. Verify coverage via WCAIS (PA workers’ compensation database).

How to verify in Upper Uwchlan: ask for the contractor’s EPA Lead-Safe certificate (when pre-1978 paint disturbance applies), a copy of insurance certificate naming your property address, and workers’ comp proof/exemption documentation. Then confirm their technical credibility by reviewing their IICRC documentation (or training records) and make sure the scope clearly separates what is non-permitted emergency drying versus what will require permits during repairs.
