Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, water damage restoration and mold work are shaped by multiple rules that overlap industry standards, waste disposal requirements, and (in some situations) lead-safety laws. Pennsylvania does not require a general contractor licence at the state level, but homeowners should still treat documentation and compliance as part of professional workmanship—especially in Ridley Park where many homes were built before 1980.

The baseline framework most insurers and reputable firms follow is the IICRC S500 standard for water damage and IICRC S520 for mold. For older properties, lead safety is a major compliance point: under the EPA Lead RRP Rule, pre-1978 homes that have lead paint surfaces disturbed must use an EPA Certified Renovator and contractors must be EPA Lead-Safe Certified. For Category 3 jobs (sewage backup), PA DEP Chapter 78 governs biohazardous/sewage waste disposal methods and handling. Building permits can also be required locally—especially for structural repairs, electrical/plumbing work, or removal of significant plaster/drywall that affects load-bearing elements.

Homeowners should verify workers’ compensation coverage as well. Pennsylvania requires employers to carry workers’ compensation; you can confirm coverage or exemption status using WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database).

What to ask for (before work starts): IICRC certification details, certificate of insurance naming the homeowner’s address, EPA Lead-Safe Certification for pre-1978 homes, and PA workers’ comp proof or exemption documentation.

How Ridley Park homeowners verify step-by-step: (1) Check IICRC certification records for the lead technician/crew lead, (2) request certificates of insurance and confirm coverage in writing, (3) confirm workers’ compensation on WCAIS (or obtain the exemption letter), and (4) if lead disturbance is possible, confirm EPA Lead-Safe Certification before any demolition of surfaces that may contain lead paint.

What is Category 3 (black water) damage?
