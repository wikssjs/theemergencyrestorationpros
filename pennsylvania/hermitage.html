Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, there generally isn’t a single “state water damage restoration license” that works like a general contractor license, but your project still has to follow overlapping rules for safety, environmental handling, and regulated work. Restoration crews in Hermitage typically follow industry standards—especially when insurers want documentation—while also complying with disposal requirements for sewage and any lead-related rules for older homes.

Here are the key compliance areas homeowners should understand:

  • IICRC S500 (water damage) and S520 (mold) set the industry baseline that many insurers expect for proper drying and mold work.
  • EPA Lead RRP Rule: If the home was built before 1978 and the work will disturb painted surfaces, contractors must have appropriate EPA Lead-Safe certification and use required lead-safe practices. You can verify certified renovators using certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov.
  • PA DEP Chapter 78: governs disposal of biohazardous/sewage waste for Category 3 projects (what gets removed and how it’s handled matters).
  • Building permits: typically needed for structural repairs, replacement that affects load-bearing elements, and electrical/plumbing work performed as part of repairs. For purely drying and removal of water-damaged materials, permits are often not required, but repairs afterward may require them.
  • Workers’ compensation: Pennsylvania requires employers to carry workers’ compensation coverage; verify or confirm an exemption through WCAIS.

What usually DOES require a permit (examples): electrical work, plumbing work, structural repairs, or any repair that involves load-bearing modifications. What typically does NOT: emergency drying/extraction itself, moisture mapping, and controlled demolition limited strictly to mitigation (though final repairs may trigger permits).

To verify credentials in Hermitage, ask for: (a) IICRC credentials/certificates for the lead technician or project lead, (b) certificate of insurance naming your address or showing general liability coverage, (c) EPA Lead-Safe certification for pre-1978 homes when disturbance is required, and (d) workers’ comp proof or exemption documentation. Then confirm workers’ comp status through the PA WCAIS record and verify lead certification via EPA’s registry before work starts.

Water damage class and category — what Hermitage homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Understanding these distinctions helps homeowners in Hermitage evaluate contractor recommendations, anticipate why some jobs require demolition or mold remediation, and make better salvage-versus-replace decisions.

In Hermitage’s housing stock—where about 62.8% of homes were built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024)—Class 3 and Class 4 damage is more common after pipe bursts, slow leaks, or repeated wetting. Older plaster walls and hardwood flooring can absorb and hold moisture longer than modern drywall and many floating-floor systems. That means drying isn’t just “get the water out”—it’s measuring moisture movement through materials and drying until structural materials return to acceptable levels.

Here’s a practical cost example: if you catch a Category 1 supply-line leak early, you may stay in the lower extraction and emergency drying range (for instance, $800–$6,000 depending on affected area). But if the same leak becomes Class 3—where water saturates insulation, walls, and the ceiling downward—drying often extends and can require more monitoring and equipment, pushing the job toward the higher bands (up to $1,500–$22,000 depending on how much demolition and remediation is triggered by time and category).
