Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, water damage and mold remediation work is governed by overlapping requirements rather than one single “permit.” While Pennsylvania does not require a general contractor license at the state level, certain restoration activities trigger standards and oversight that affect how contractors do the job and what paperwork you should expect.

Industry baseline: Most insurers and restoration best practices reference IICRC standards—S500 for water damage and S520 for mold—because these define how drying and mold work should be performed and documented.

Lead safety: If your home was built before 1978, the EPA Lead Renovation, Repair and Painting (Lead RRP) Rule applies. Contractors must be EPA Lead-Safe Certified and have the required certified renovator practices when disturbing lead-painted surfaces.

Category 3 disposal: PA DEP Chapter 78 governs sewage/biohazard waste disposal for Category 3 projects. That impacts how crews remove contaminated porous materials and where waste is taken.

Building permits: In South Hanover and across Dauphin County, permits are typically required for structural repairs and certain reconstruction tasks—like drywall replacement in quantity, framing changes, and any electrical/plumbing work. Drying and extraction are usually handled without a building permit, but reconstruction scope can require municipal sign-off.

Workers’ compensation: Employers in Pennsylvania must carry workers’ compensation; verify via WCAIS (PA workers’ comp database) or a documented exemption.

To verify a contractor, ask for (a) IICRC certificate(s), (b) an insurance certificate naming your address/interest, (c) EPA Lead-Safe Certification for pre-1978 homes, and (d) PA workers’ comp proof or exemption. Then confirm details using online registries (EPA Lead Certified Renovator search for lead credentials, WCAIS for workers’ comp, and certificate-of-insurance documentation directly from the insurer).

Water damage class and category — what South Hanover homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Restoration contractors classify damage using Class (how far the water has spread and how much material is saturated) and Category (how contaminated the water is). Understanding both helps South Hanover homeowners judge whether a recommendation to remove drywall, lift flooring, or run extended drying cycles is necessary—or just a sales pitch.

Because about 35.5% of South Hanover housing stock predates 1980, we commonly see situations where Class 3 and Class 4 water absorption is more likely after pipe bursts or slow leaks. Older plaster and hardwood floors often absorb and hold moisture differently than newer all-drywall assemblies, which can change the “real” drying time. In practice, that means one contractor may propose extraction only, while another—following IICRC S500 principles—might recommend moisture mapping, careful removal of wetted materials, and a longer LGR drying schedule to protect framing and preserve finishes.

Here’s the key decision point: Category drives health risk and protocol, while Class drives drying complexity. For example, a supply line break that affects a small area may still be manageable (Class 1), with costs closer to the $1,200–$5,200 extraction/emergency drying band. But if the same leak saturates walls and subfloor (moving toward Class 2–3), the project can shift toward extended structural drying—often 3–5 days or more for documentation and verification—even when the water itself is “clean.”

For homeowners, the goal is not to “buy more work,” but to understand what’s justified by how much material is saturated and what cleanup level is required by the water source—especially during Pennsylvania’s wet seasons and freeze–thaw swings.
