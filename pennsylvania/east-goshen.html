Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, restoration work is handled under several overlapping requirements, and what needs a permit usually depends on the repair scope—not just the water cleanup. While Pennsylvania does not require a general contractor license at the state level, the work itself may be regulated through (1) industry standards used by insurers and carried out on-site, (2) environmental rules for waste disposal, and (3) municipal permit requirements when you start rebuilding or altering building systems.

Here are the most relevant rules for East Goshen homeowners: IICRC S500 (water damage) and S520 (mold) are the industry baseline that many insurers expect. If the home is pre-1978 and a contractor disturbs paint surfaces (for example, cutting out water-damaged drywall/plaster), the EPA Lead RRP Rule may apply—pre-1978 projects generally require an EPA Certified Renovator on site. For sewage and biohazard disposal, PA DEP Chapter 78 governs sewage/biohazardous waste handling for Category 3 projects. For rebuilding, permits are typically required for structural repairs, drywall replacement that affects load-bearing elements, and any electrical/plumbing work done in conjunction with restoration—confirm with East Goshen Township or the relevant municipal office before work begins.

Step-by-step credential checks: (1) verify IICRC certification (or project lead certification) by requesting the IICRC credentials and training dates; (2) confirm insurance via a current certificate of insurance naming your address as additional insured where applicable; (3) check EPA Lead-Safe Certification for pre-1978 homes using the certified renovator lookup at certifiedrenovatorsearch.epa.gov; (4) request proof of Pennsylvania workers’ compensation coverage or an exemption—verify employers using the WCAIS workers’ comp database when available.

Ask for: (a) IICRC certificate for the water/mold work lead, (b) COI showing property-address coverage, (c) EPA Lead-Safe Certification proof (when pre-1978 surfaces will be disturbed), and (d) PA workers’ comp proof or exemption documentation.

Water damage class and category — what East Goshen homeowners need to know

Not all water damage is treated the same way. Restoration contractors classify damage by both CLASS (how extensively the water has spread and absorbed into materials) and CATEGORY (how contaminated the water is). Understanding these distinctions helps East Goshen homeowners evaluate recommendations, spot why two bids can be different, and make smarter salvage-versus-replace choices. In East Goshen’s older housing stock—43.0% built before 1980—Class 3 and 4 damage is more common after plumbing failures because plaster walls and hardwood floors can absorb and hold moisture differently than newer assemblies. When saturation extends into base plates, insulation, and subfloors, drying becomes slower and more equipment-intensive, and the clearance testing and monitoring time increases.

Category also matters. Clean-water incidents (Category 1) typically focus on extraction and controlled drying. Grey water (Category 2) requires enhanced sanitation practices. Black water (Category 3) requires full biohazard protocols, antimicrobial treatment, containment practices, and careful disposal of contaminated porous materials.

Here’s a practical dollar example: if a leak is truly limited to a small area (Class 1), homeowners often fit within the lower end of the $1,200–$4,200 extraction and emergency drying band. If the same leak becomes Class 3 (water absorbed into insulation and materials), the project commonly shifts toward multi-day drying and can move within the broader $1,200–$12,000 structural drying range because drying time, monitoring, and the need to address hidden wet materials increase. The key is that classification drives both equipment planning and the total scope of restoration.
